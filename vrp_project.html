Project Overview

This project explores the Variance Risk Premium (VRP) - the empirical phenomenon where implied volatility (from options markets) systematically exceeds realized volatility (from actual returns). The goal was to investigate whether this premium exists in S&P 500 data and whether timing mechanisms add value beyond passive short-variance exposure.

Using 15 years of S&P 500 and VIX data, I built a proxy framework to simulate variance swap payoffs and tested three different strategies: a baseline approach, an improved version with exit rules, and an always-on exposure benchmark.

Methodology

What is VRP?

The variance risk premium represents the gap between:

  • Implied Variance: Market expectations backed out from VIX (30-day implied volatility)
  • Realized Variance: Actual volatility calculated from squared log returns
Key Hypothesis: If implied variance consistently exceeds realized variance, selling variance (being short volatility) should generate positive returns on average, compensating investors for bearing tail risk.

Approach

Since I didn't have access to actual variance swap data, I used a proxy methodology:

  • VIX as proxy for 30-day implied volatility
  • 21-day rolling window for realized volatility calculations
  • Simulated short variance positions with entry/exit rules
  • Compared three strategies: Baseline, Improved (with exits), and Always-On

Risk Controls Implemented

  • VIX threshold filters to avoid entering during extreme volatility
  • Stop-loss mechanisms based on cumulative P&L
  • Position sizing based on normalized VRP z-scores
  • Early exit triggers when VRP turns negative

1. VRP Exists in the Data

Implied variance exceeded realized variance in roughly 65% of periods, confirming the theoretical prediction that variance sellers earn a premium for bearing tail risk. The premium was particularly pronounced during calm market periods (2012-2019).

2. Implementation Details Matter Critically

Critical Discovery: The initial backtest had two significant bugs:
  • Lookahead bias in exit logic: Exit decisions were using future information
  • Incorrect variance annualization: When positions closed early, realized variance was being annualized incorrectly, using the full holding period instead of actual days held
These bugs inflated the Sharpe ratio by approximately 60%. After fixes, results became much more realistic.

3. Timing Adds Limited Alpha

The most surprising finding: being always short variance captured ~90% of the total returns. The sophisticated entry/exit rules primarily helped with:

  • Reducing maximum drawdown (from -60% to -45%)
  • Improving risk-adjusted metrics slightly
  • Avoiding some of the worst periods (March 2020, volmageddon)

But they didn't materially increase absolute returns. This suggests the edge is primarily in the beta exposure to VRP, not in clever timing.

4. Crisis Periods Dominate Risk

The strategy's worst performance occurred during:

  • March 2020 (COVID): Single worst day lost 60% of cumulative gains
  • Feb 2018 (Volmageddon): Rapid VIX spike caused significant drawdown
  • Q4 2018: Fed tightening concerns created persistent volatility

Technical Challenges

Debugging the Backtest

The most valuable learning came from debugging the initial implementation:

  • Lookahead bias: Exit logic was checking if VRP_t+1 < 0 instead of VRP_t < 0
  • Annualization error: Used formula RV = sqrt(252/H * sum(returns^2)) even when holding period was less than H days
  • Verification: Cross-checked results against crisis periods manually to ensure P&L made sense

Feature Engineering

  • Calculated 21-day rolling realized volatility
  • Computed VRP in variance units (IV¬≤ - RV¬≤)
  • Created z-score normalized signals using 126-day rolling window
  • Generated binary entry/exit flags based on VRP regime

Limitations

1. Proxy Methodology

  • VIX is not a tradeable variance swap strike
  • No access to actual option surfaces or variance swap curves
  • Cannot model proper mark-to-market on early exits
  • Missing term structure, skew, and convexity effects

2. P&L in Variance Points, Not Dollars

  • Results shown in variance points per unit notional
  • No margin framework or capital requirements modeled
  • Execution costs, dealer spreads, and slippage not included
  • Cannot directly translate to portfolio returns without sizing assumptions

3. Tail Risk

  • Short volatility strategies can experience catastrophic losses
  • March 2020 and February 2018 demonstrated this clearly
  • Small implementation errors can drastically change outcomes

Lessons Learned

  • Verify, verify, verify: Small coding errors can create massive fake alpha in volatility strategies
  • Crisis tests are essential: Always manually check how strategies performed in known crisis periods
  • Beta vs alpha: Most "sophisticated" timing overlays don't beat simple exposure in volatility selling
  • Proxy limitations: Without proper data, conclusions must be appropriately hedged
  • Documentation matters: Clear explanation of assumptions and limitations is as important as the results
